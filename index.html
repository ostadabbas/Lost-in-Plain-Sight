Overview

The essence of video lies in pixel dynamics: motion, state transitions, and the flow of visual information across frames. In this position paper, we argue that recent progress in video understanding is measured by benchmarks and protocols that can be solved without reliably perceiving spatiotemporal evidence, rewarding language-driven plausibility over video-grounded inference.

The Illusion of Success: Benchmark Alignment and Architectural Biases

A Benchmark Perspective

If a video understanding benchmark can be solved without seeing the video, it is not measuring video understanding; it is measuring language reasoning. A large proportion of widely used benchmark questions are LLM-answerable, and additional subsets are semantic-only, solvable even when frame order is disrupted.

An Architecture Perspective

Current architectures are effective for scaling, but they also systematically compress or defer temporal information, biasing the system toward language-driven inference. In this regime, what is present is represented better than what changes.

Failure Mode 1: Static-Cue Dominance

Failure Mode 1

Video LLMs can appear temporally competent while systematically underusing the very signal that makes video distinct: dynamic evolution in pixels. In this failure mode, appearance and context outweigh spatiotemporal evidence.

Failure Mode 2: Prior-driven Temporal Hallucination

Failure Mode 2

When the visual evidence in a video is subtle or counterintuitive, Video LLMs often do not default to uncertainty. Instead, they hallucinate a false reality: canonical event scripts substitute for evidence-based state tracking.

Diagnostic Walkthrough

To move beyond aggregate benchmarks, these examples highlight a fundamental inability to distinguish specific motion primitives even in simple scenes, and a tendency to complete likely narratives when evidence is counterintuitive.

Overview of Video LLM Failure Modes

Static-cue dominance and prior-driven temporal hallucination are coupled failure modes.

Alternative Views

Reliance on static semantics and world knowledge is a rational and efficient design choice.

Static cues are a powerful first approximation. However, predictiveness should not be conflated with understanding. A system that cannot reliably abandon shortcuts when they become misleading is not truly video-aware.

Dropping language backbones and reverting to video-native architectures is a false dichotomy.

The path forward is agentic and evidence-gated: use language-model reasoning while enforcing explicit consistency checks against observed spatiotemporal evidence.

Call to Action: Towards Dynamically-Aware Video Understanding

Representational Shifts

The fundamental unit of video understanding should not be static objects, but transformations over time.

Structurally Enforced Spatiotemporal Grounding

Generation should be explicitly constrained by spatiotemporal visual evidence.

Evaluation and Benchmark Standards

Benchmarks should satisfy temporal sensitivity, evidence localization, and pixel-level verifiability. Recognizing what did not happen is as critical as recognizing what did.
